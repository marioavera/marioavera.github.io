Report quality

What makes a police report narrative defensible

Most of a report is fields. The narrative is the part that gets read aloud in a courtroom, line by line, by someone whose job is to find the seam in it. These are the properties that hold up when that happens — and the conditions in the field that quietly erode them.

A report serves several audiences before it ever reaches a courtroom. A supervisor reads it for completeness. Records reads it for coding. A prosecutor reads it to decide whether there is a case. An insurance adjuster or a records requester may read it years later. Each of them is reading for something different.

But only one audience reads it adversarially. Defense counsel reads the narrative looking for the place where the officer's account and the officer's testimony do not line up, or where the report says something the officer cannot now explain. Everything below is about surviving that reading.

1. Personal knowledge

Every sentence in a narrative implicitly claims a source. The officer saw it, heard it, smelled it, measured it, or was told it by someone identified in the report. A narrative that contains a statement the officer cannot source is a liability, and it is usually the first thing found on cross.

This is the property most easily lost without anyone noticing. It erodes when a report is assembled from several inputs at once — dispatch notes, another officer's account, a partner's recollection, a body camera review — and the boundaries between those sources dissolve in the writing. The sentence still reads well. It just no longer has a clean owner.

The question is never whether the sentence is true. It is whether the officer can say how he knows it.

2. Observation separated from conclusion

"The driver was impaired" is a conclusion. "The driver's eyes were watery and bloodshot, his speech was slurred, and I detected an odor of an alcoholic beverage emanating from his breath as he spoke" is a set of observations that supports one.

The second version is harder to attack because there is nothing to attack — each element is something the officer perceived and can describe. The first version invites the only question that matters: based on what?

Conclusions are not forbidden. An officer is entitled to form them, and in many contexts is required to. The requirement is that the observations underneath them appear in the report, so that the conclusion is shown as a product of the officer's perception rather than asserted on its own.

3. Contemporaneous capture

Memory does not fail all at once. It degrades in a specific and predictable pattern: the narrative arc of the call survives, and the details attach themselves to it afterward. Sequence compresses. Times round themselves off. Quotes smooth into paraphrase. Descriptions drift toward the typical.

The officer is not aware of any of this happening. The report written three hours later at a station computer reads as confidently as the one written at the scene. It is simply less accurate, in ways that only surface when the report is compared against video, another officer's account, or a witness's memory.

Anything captured while the officer is still standing there — a time, an exact phrase, a plate, a position, a spontaneous statement — is fixed. Anything reconstructed afterward is reconstruction, however good the officer is.

4. Attribution

Reports routinely contain information the officer did not personally observe: what a witness said, what dispatch relayed, what a complainant reported, what another unit found. None of that is a problem as long as the report says whose information it is.

It becomes a problem when the attribution is dropped and the relayed information reads as the officer's own observation. That is the difference between a report that describes what was reported to the officer and one that appears to claim the officer saw it.

5. The officer has to be able to testify to it

This is the test that contains all the others. Every sentence in the narrative is something the officer may be asked to explain, under oath, possibly years later, with the report in front of him and a copy in the hands of someone looking for inconsistency.

A sentence the officer cannot explain the origin of is worse than a sentence that was never written. The gap is not neutral — it is an opening, and it applies to the rest of the report by extension. An officer who cannot account for one paragraph has a credibility problem with all of them.

What this means for how reports get written

Most of these properties are not lost through carelessness. They are lost through the conditions under which reports are actually produced: at the end of a shift, from notes taken under pressure, hours after the call, on a machine in a room full of other people finishing their own paperwork.

Two things reliably protect them:

  • Capture as close to the event as possible. Not the polished narrative — the raw material. Times, exact words, positions, descriptions, plate numbers. Whatever is fixed while the officer is still there does not have to be reconstructed later.
  • Structure that keeps sources distinct. When observation, statement, and relayed information are captured in separate places rather than merged into prose at the moment of writing, attribution survives by default rather than by the officer's discipline at 0300.

Neither of these is about writing style. They are about when and how the underlying material gets recorded — which is a workflow problem before it is a writing problem.

A note on tools

Any tool that touches report writing should be evaluated against the list above rather than against how much faster it makes the paperwork. The relevant questions are narrow:

  • After using it, can the officer still source every sentence in the narrative?
  • Does it preserve the distinction between what was observed and what was concluded?
  • Does it move capture closer to the event, or further from it?
  • Does attribution survive the process, or does it get smoothed away?
  • Can the officer explain, on the stand, how each part of the document came to exist?

A tool that improves speed while weakening any of these has moved the cost rather than removed it. The paperwork gets done sooner and the problem shows up later, in a setting where it is considerably more expensive.
